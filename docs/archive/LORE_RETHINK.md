# Lore Rethink — Netheron, God of Death

*This is an alternative take on Netheron's role and the Sundering. Compare with the current version in LORE.md to find what works best.*

---

## The Current Version (LORE.md)

Netheron was the seventh deity of the Eternal Court. He believed mortals were enslaved by their dependence on the gods. He proposed the Severance — a complete break between divine and mortal. The Six refused. He acted alone. The Sundering shattered all seven deities, including himself. His fragmented essence leaks out as the Hollow Wastes — a dead god's corpse poisoning the continent. The corruption has no will or intelligence behind it.

The Cult of the Hollow worships him as "the Liberator" — a god who sacrificed himself to free mortals from divine tyranny.

---

## The Rethink

### Netheron Had a Function

Netheron wasn't just "the seventh god." He was the **god of death** — the deity who maintained the boundary between the world of the living and the world of the dead. Every soul that passed, every cycle of life and decay, every ghost that didn't linger — that was Netheron's work.

The other six gods created life, growth, light, knowledge, the sea, and war. Netheron's domain was the other side of all of that — endings, transitions, the quiet dark that follows. Without death, there is no meaning to life. The Eternal Court needed him. The world needed him.

But mortals didn't see it that way.

### Why Mortals Feared Him

People prayed to Ferros when they needed tools. They thanked Sylvana for the harvest. They asked Solara for warmth. They begged Korrath for victory. They sought Lunara for wisdom. They offered to Nereia for safe voyages.

Nobody prayed to Netheron — they **cursed** him. When a child died, they cursed his name. When plague came, they blamed him. When the elderly passed in the night, people spat at his shrines. He was necessary. He was essential. And he was hated for it.

The other six were loved, celebrated, adorned with temples and festivals. Netheron's shrines were avoided. His priests were shunned. His name was spoken as a curse word.

### What Broke Him

This went on for thousands of years. A god defined by his function, performing it faithfully, and receiving nothing but fear and hatred in return. The other six did nothing to change this — they enjoyed their worship, their festivals, their golden temples. They needed Netheron but they didn't stand up for him.

Maybe it was jealousy. Maybe it was loneliness. Maybe it was a genuine philosophical conviction that the system was unjust. Probably it was all three, tangled together the way real motivations always are.

Netheron didn't propose a calm "Severance." He **raged**. He demanded the Court acknowledge his role. When they refused — when they told him to accept his place, that someone had to bear the burden — he decided that if mortals wanted to live without death's shepherd, they could try.

### The War and the Sundering

The Sundering wasn't a spell or a ritual. It was a **war among gods.** The Six fought Netheron. The battle tore the fabric of reality. Mountains cracked. Seas boiled. The sky went dark for a year.

Netheron fell. Whether the Six killed him or he chose to die out of spite, or whether the sheer violence of the battle destroyed all seven of them — nobody knows. The result was the same: all seven deities shattered into fragments, their consciousness scattered across the world.

The old kingdom, caught in the crossfire of a divine war, was annihilated.

### The Consequence — Death Without a Shepherd

With Netheron gone, **his function stopped being performed.** The boundary between the living and the dead began to break down. Not immediately — it took millennia for the damage to become visible. The boundary held on its own for a long time, like a dam without a keeper. But slowly, inevitably, it cracked.

The Hollow Wastes are the place where the boundary is thinnest — where Netheron's essence was most concentrated, where the war was fiercest. The dead rise there not because of malice or necromancy, but because **death itself is broken**. There is no one to guide souls across. There is no one to keep the dead from wandering back.

The Wastes didn't spread for thousands of years because the boundary, though unmanaged, was still mostly intact. But ~150-200 years ago, the cracks reached a tipping point. Now the boundary is actively failing. The Wastes are growing. The dead are walking further and further from the epicenter.

The whispering that drove the watchtower garrison mad? Not Netheron's intelligence reaching out. Just the boundary between worlds growing so thin that the voices of the dead bleed through.

### How This Changes the Factions

**The Cult of the Hollow** — their philosophy shifts. They don't worship Netheron as a "liberator" who chose to free mortals. They worship him as a **martyr** — a god who was despised for doing the most essential work, who was driven to madness by ingratitude, and whose destruction proves that mortals didn't deserve the gods' protection. Their argument: "You hated death. You cursed the one god who kept death orderly. And now you have what you wanted — a world without him. How's that working out?"

**The Church of the Radiant One** — their doctrine takes on a different flavor. They teach that the Radiant One (the "true" god) will restore the boundary when mortals prove worthy. The Sundering was punishment for ingratitude — mortals should have honored all seven equally. The Church's anti-magic stance: magic use further destabilizes the already-damaged boundary between worlds.

**The Thornveil Pact** — they honor ALL the old gods, including Netheron. They maintain that death was sacred, that Netheron's role was honored in the old ways, and that the Sundering happened because mortals forgot to respect the dark as well as the light. Their Primal wards aren't just magical barriers — they're **funeral rites** on a massive scale, trying to do what Netheron used to do: guide the dead back across, maintain the boundary, keep the worlds separate.

**Halldora** — she was alive when it happened. She SAW the war. She remembers Netheron before the madness took him — she might have even known him. She carries the guilt of an entire civilization that took death for granted. Her research isn't just academic — it's atonement.

**Inquisitor Maren** — her argument becomes even more compelling. "Every time you use magic, you thin the boundary further. Every spell, every potion, every blessing — you're widening the cracks that Netheron's fall created. The only way to let the boundary heal is to stop poking at it."

### The Dragons

If the boundary between the living and the dead is breaking down, the Sundering Scars are the places where the two worlds overlap most intensely. The dragon eggs aren't just crystallized Aether — they're **something from between worlds**, formed in the space where life and death meet. They are genuinely unprecedented — not creatures of the living world or the dead one, but something new. Something born from the boundary itself.

This makes them even more mysterious. Are they alive? Are they dead? Are they something else entirely? Nobody knows. Not even Halldora.

### The Central Question

The current lore asks: "Can the corruption be stopped?"

This version asks something deeper: **"Can death be fixed?"**

Netheron is gone. His function is unperformed. The boundary is failing. Can mortals find a way to maintain it themselves? Can the fragments of the six gods be used somehow? Can the dragons — born from the broken boundary — become the new shepherds of death?

Or is the world simply dying, slowly, because mortals killed the one god they couldn't live without?

## What Stays the Same

- The Sundering was thousands of years ago
- The deities are fragmented, not sleeping or hiding
- The Hollow Wastes are the epicenter
- The Wastes started expanding ~150-200 years ago
- The Dominion, Thornveil, Corsairs, and Cult exist as described
- Halldora witnessed the Sundering and is ancient
- Dragons are born from the Scars
- The Church's anti-magic doctrine
- Free Settlers as the player's faction

## What Changes

- Netheron has a sympathetic backstory — feared, not evil
- The Sundering was a divine war, not a philosophical disagreement
- The Wastes are caused by ABSENCE (no death god) rather than PRESENCE (leaking essence)
- The undead have a clearer explanation — the boundary is broken
- The Cult becomes more sympathetic — they're angry on Netheron's behalf
- The central mystery shifts from "stop the corruption" to "fix death itself"
- Halldora's motivation becomes personal guilt, not just academic curiosity
