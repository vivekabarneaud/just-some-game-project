# The Shattered Realm

*A novel set in the world of Valenheart*

---

## Chapter 1: Cheap Land

The Dominion sells the south as opportunity. The posters in every town square say the same thing: *Free land for the bold. Build your future beyond the frontier.* They show green hills, fat livestock, golden wheat. They do not mention why the land is free.

I should have asked.

We arrived in late spring — forty-three of us, give or take the ones who turned back at the river crossing. Farmers, mostly. A few tradesmen. One woman who claimed to be a healer but spent the entire journey north complaining about the mud. A blacksmith named Torben who spoke maybe six words in three weeks, all of them about iron.

The site we picked was good enough. A hill with a clear view south, a creek that ran clean, and a stand of oaks that would give us lumber for a season. I remember standing on that hill the first evening, watching the sun go down over a treeline that stretched to the horizon, thinking: *This is it. No landlord. No tithe collector. No Church inspector asking why you own a book.*

We lit a fire. Someone produced a bottle of something Feldgrund — one of the hill-dwarves, Bardin, a round man with forearms like hams who'd brought three barrels of ale and not a single change of clothes. He poured for everyone. "To cheap land," he said, and we drank, and the fire crackled, and the stars came out one by one like they'd been waiting for permission.

That was the last uncomplicated evening we had.

---

Building a settlement from nothing is exactly as romantic as it sounds, which is to say not at all. The first week was blisters, arguments about where to dig the latrine, and the discovery that our one experienced carpenter had lied about being experienced. The town hall — if you could call a timber frame with a canvas roof a town hall — took four days. The houses took longer, because everyone had opinions about where theirs should go and nobody had opinions about helping with someone else's.

I kept the ledger. Someone had to. I wrote down what we had, what we needed, and what we'd run out of first. The answer was always the same: patience.

But we had Torben, and Torben could make nails. And we had Bardin, and Bardin could make ale. And between nails and ale, you can build almost anything, because the nails hold the walls up and the ale makes you forget how badly you built them.

By the second week, we had something that looked — from a generous distance, in poor light — like a village.

---

The adventurers came later.

I hadn't planned for that. The Dominion posters didn't mention that the frontier attracts a certain kind of person: the kind that carries a sword and knows how to use it. They drift in from everywhere, drawn by rumours of work and the absence of law. Some are genuine heroes. Some are running from something. Most are both.

The first was a woman named Amara. Zah'kari — you could tell from the gold dust in her braids and the way she stood, like the ground owed her an apology for being uneven. She walked into camp on a Tuesday, looked at our sad little palisade, and said: "You need a guild."

"We need a well," I said.

"You need a guild," she repeated, "because the things that live in these woods don't care about your well."

I didn't ask what things. I should have asked what things.

Instead I asked what a guild cost. She said food, lodging, and a noticeboard. I gave her a corner of the town hall and a plank of oak. She nailed it to the wall and wrote CONTRACTS across the top in charcoal.

That was the Adventurer's Guild. It grew from there.

---

They came from everywhere. That's the thing about the frontier — it doesn't care where you're from, only what you can do.

There was Lucien, a Hauts-Cieux elf who spoke three languages, quoted pre-Sundering poetry, and visibly flinched every time someone used the word *ruins*. He wouldn't say why he'd come down from the sky-cities. "The archives are complete," he said once, when I pressed. "There is nothing left to catalogue." He said it like it was the saddest sentence in the world.

There was Sigrid Frostwind, a Nordveld shieldmaiden who'd been a Thornveil Ranger before something happened that she didn't talk about. She knew the forest. She knew the old ward-signs carved into the boundary stones. She knew which mushrooms were poisonous and which were merely unpleasant. She called the Church "those lunatics in the gold hats" and honoured all seven gods, including the one nobody names.

"All seven?" I asked.

She gave me a look that could have frozen the creek. "There were seven. Pretending there weren't doesn't make it true."

I didn't know what she meant. Not then.

There was Kofi, a Zah'kari griot — a keeper of stories, he said. He'd followed a tale north, one that didn't have an ending yet. When I asked which tale, he smiled and said: "This one." He had an infuriating habit of being right about things he had no business knowing.

There was Durin Stonefist, a Khazdurim dwarf with hands like shovels and eyes that went somewhere else when it got quiet. I made the mistake once of asking about the deep holds — the sealed tunnels in the Ironspine Mountains. Every other dwarf I'd met waved it off. Durin didn't wave it off. He set down his ale, stood up, and walked outside. When he came back, twenty minutes later, neither of us mentioned it. But his hands were shaking, and not from the cold.

And there was Mei, a Tianzhou cartographer who was technically mapping the western continent but spent most of her time staring south through a brass spyglass at the treeline. "The maps say forest," she told me, frowning. "But the colour is wrong."

"Wrong how?"

"Trees are green."

I looked south. The treeline was green. Mostly. If you didn't look too hard at the edges, where the canopy thinned and the branches went the colour of old bone.

"It's probably fine," I said.

She didn't answer.

## Chapter 2: The Ruins on the Hill

I sent the scouts south on the third week. Not because I was worried — I wasn't, not yet — but because the ledger said we needed stone, and the nearest quarry was wherever we found one.

Amara went, and Sigrid, and a young Ashwick lad named Finn who'd been a soldier before he'd been a deserter. They were gone two days.

They came back with a map, a bag of interesting rocks, and a look I didn't like.

"There are ruins," Amara said. "Half a day south. On a hill."

"Ruins" on the frontier isn't unusual. The Sundering — whatever that was, exactly; everyone has a different story — happened thousands of years ago, and the land is littered with the bones of whatever came before. Broken walls. Collapsed cellars. The occasional foundation that's too square to be natural. You learn to ignore them the way you learn to ignore the odd sounds the forest makes at night.

But these weren't ancient.

"Dominion," Sigrid said. "Standard garrison layout. Cots, an armoury, a command post. Maybe fifty years old."

"And they left?"

"In a hurry." She set a leather-bound journal on my desk. It was water-damaged, the pages swollen and stuck together. Most of it was illegible. The last few entries were not.

*Day 41. The whispering is worse at night. Corporal Daven says it's the wind. It is not the wind. The wind does not know your name.*

*Day 44. Maren heard her mother's voice coming from the well. Her mother has been dead for six years.*

*Day 47. The whispering won't stop. Maren heard her name. We are leaving at dawn.*

Below that, in a different hand — shakier, the ink pressed hard enough to tear the paper:

*They didn't all leave.*

I closed the journal.

"Could be anything," I said. "Garrison isolation. Bad water. Desertion stories always sound dramatic after the fact."

Amara looked at Sigrid. Sigrid looked at me. Neither of them said anything, which was worse than if they'd argued.

"We also found bones," Finn said quietly. "In the tower. Not old bones."

The room was very still.

"Right," I said. "We'll send a team. A proper one. Armed."

I wrote it on the noticeboard that evening. The first real contract the guild ever posted. The ink was barely dry before Amara had a team assembled.

I watched them leave at dawn, four figures with weapons and packs, heading south toward a hill where fifty Dominion soldiers had been stationed and something had made them run.

The Dominion posters hadn't mentioned that either.

## Chapter 3: Where the Birds Don't Sing

They found what the journal described: a dark treeline to the south. Past the ruins, past the scrub hills, the forest began — and then, at some point that was hard to pin down, it stopped being a forest and became something else.

The trees were still standing. That was the unsettling part. From a distance it looked normal. It was only when you got close that you saw the wood was grey, brittle, cold to the touch. No bark beetles. No birds. No moss, no lichen, no fungus — nothing that feeds on dead wood, because even the things that eat death wouldn't touch this.

The ground was soft. Not muddy — *soft*, like walking on something that had recently stopped breathing.

They went half a mile in. That's when the air changed.

Sigrid described it later, sitting by the fire with a mug she wasn't drinking from: "Copper on the tongue. Pressure behind the eyes. Like being underwater, except you can breathe." She paused. "And the voices."

"Voices."

"Fragments. Sentences that start and don't finish. Too quiet to make out, but you *almost* can. That's the worst part — the *almost*. Your brain keeps trying to hear them, and the harder you listen, the closer they feel."

Finn said one of them sounded like his sister, who was alive and well in Ashwick.

Kofi said nothing at all, which for a man who made his living with words was the loudest silence I'd ever heard.

They pulled back. At the treeline, looking south, you could see it: miles and miles of dead forest, stretching to the horizon in every direction. A grey sea where green should be.

"It's not local," Amara said. "It's not a blight or a disease. It's a *line*. On one side, life. On the other..." She trailed off. "Whatever that is."

I looked at Sigrid. "You know what it is."

She was quiet for a long time.

"In Nordveld," she said finally, "the elders call it the Breath of the Shepherd. The place where the boundary between the living and the dead wears thin." She stared into the fire. "I thought it was up north. Near the old ward-line. A hundred miles from here at least."

"And?"

"And it's not a hundred miles from here. It's half a day's walk." She met my eyes. "It's closer than it should be. Which means it's moving."

Nobody spoke.

The fire crackled. An owl called from the oaks, and the sound was so normal, so *alive*, that it felt like a rebuke.

"The Dominion garrison," I said slowly. "Fifty soldiers. The whispering. The bones in the tower."

Sigrid nodded. "They built their outpost on the edge of the boundary without knowing what it was. And the boundary came for them."

I sat with that for a while.

"The Dominion sold us this land," I said.

"Yes."

"They knew."

"Probably. Or they didn't care. With the Dominion, it amounts to the same thing."

I looked south, toward the treeline that was green on one side and grey on the other, and I thought about the posters in the town squares. *Free land for the bold.* I thought about forty-three settlers who'd come here for a fresh start, who were sleeping right now in houses held together with Torben's nails, who had no idea that the cheap land they'd staked their futures on was cheap for a reason.

I should have asked.
